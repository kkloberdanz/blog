Convolutions - Where Physics and ML Meet

24 Sep 2020

There's a concept in both Physics and Machine Learning called Convolution. There is some debate over whether or not they are the same concept, but I would argue that they are related.

This might seem like a round about explanation, but to understand convolutions in machine learning, let's first look at what convolution means from a Physics perspective.

Starting with the setup, I'm going to plug one end of my headphone cable into my laptop, and the other I'll attach to my oscilloscope. I'll attach the probe to the left audio channel, and I'll ground the probe on the ground connector on the audio cable.

Using an online tone generator , I'll be able to generator audible waves that my oscilloscope will be able to display.

Let's start by looking at a simple sin wave. This signal is at 440 hz.

Here we have another sin wave, but this time at 330 hz

These are pretty boring waves, not much to see here. But let's take a look at what happens when we add these waves together.

t = 0:1/50:10-1/50;
x = sin(2*pi*15*t) + sin(2*pi*20*t);
plot(t,x)
y = fft(x);
f = (0:length(y)-1)*50/length(y);
plot(f,abs(y))
